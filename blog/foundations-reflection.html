Foundation Reflection


  1. What are three things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the Human Skills learning?


    2. Through the Human Skills learning, I realized that I am more self-critical than I thought.
    I often judged myself harshly when I didn't meet my own expectations, especially during coding challenges.
    I also noticed that my ego sometimes gets in the way of asking for help - I used to feel like I had to figure things out on my own to prove myself.
    Lastly, I learned that I am more empathetic than I assumed.
    When I listened deeply to others in looping exercises, I felt a strong connection and desire to support them, which helped me feel more human in a tech space.




  2. What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?


    3. Values, empathy, and self-awareness are like the foundation of being a good programmer, not just a skilled one.
    My values help guide the kind of projects I want to work on and remind me why I'm learning to code in the first place.
    Empathy lets me understand the users I'm building for - and also helps me work better with others, especially when pair programming of giving/receiving feedback.
    Self-awareness is what helps me notice when I'm stuck, when I'm frustrated, of when I need a break. It helps me take care of myself so I can keep going in a sustainable way.
    Without these human skills, learning would feel mechanical and disconnected.
    With them, I feel more grounded and connected to others and to my purpose.




  3. What has surprised you the most about the Human Skills learning?


    4. What surprised me the most was how deeply emotional the learning process can be.
    I didn't expect a coding school to ask me to reflect on my feelings, my mindset, or how I talk to myself - but it actually made a huge difference.
    I was surprised by how much my mood, stress level, and self-talk could affect how I learn.
    I also didn't expect that just listening to someone else (really listening) could feel so powerful.
    It reminded me that tech is about people, not just screens and code.
    Overall, I've come to see that building human skills is just as valuable as building technical ones.




  4. What were the most challenging aspects of the Human Skills learning?


    5. The most challenging part for me was being honest with myself - especially when reflecting on things like my mindset, emotions, and reactions.
    Sometimes it was uncomfortable to admit when I was frustrated, unmotivated, or comparing myself to others.
    Writing personal reflections in English also felt hard because I wanted to express deep thoughts, but didn't always have the right words.
    But I learned that just trying, even imperfectly, was powerful.
    The process of slowing down and checking in with myself was challenging - but also healing.




  5. Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on Human Skills learning in a web develoment Bootcamp course?


    6. Because in the real world, being a great developer isn't just about writing clean code - it's about working with people.
    Projects are built in teams, feedback needs to be shared, and communication can be the difference between success and failure.
    Human Skills help us become better collaborators, better listeners, and more resilient learners.
    Especially in tech, where things change so fast, having a growth mindset and emotional intelligence makes it easier to keep adapting and learning.
    This learning gave me tools not just for coding, but for life - and I can already tell it will help me long after the Bootcamp ends.




  6. Does the time you spent studying Human Skills learning here feel like a waste of time?
    Should you have just used that time to practice programming instead? Justify your answer.

    7. Honestly, it didn't feel like a waste at all - if anything, I feel like it made my programming journey more meaningful.
    Human Skills gave me the mental tools to deal with stress, self-doubt, and moments when I wanted to give up.
    Without that, I might have burned out or lost confidence.
    It also reminded me that tech is about people. We're building things for people and with people, so understanding ourselves and others is essential.
    Of course I want to keep practising coding - but now I can do it with more clarity empathy, and resilience. That's a huge win.
